Introduction

Synthetic Aperture Radar is a technique established in the 1950s. Carl Wiley, most viewed as the founder of SAR, started to focus on airborne radar. In 1951, he found that radar reflection from discrete objects in a passing radar beam field each had a minute Doppler, or speed, shift relative to the antenna. He could use antenna 1/100 the size of traditional radar to create detailed radar images. He applied for the patent for Simultaneous Buildup Doppler in 1954, which was considered the first SAR patent. Soon in 1960 the further study of SAR successfully won the competition to build imaging radar for the Lockheed SR-71 Blackbird --- the fastest jet in the world.

Summary

The Synthetic Aperture Radar has incredibly extensive usage. It has the ability to go through the clouds and have a peek at the Earth’s geological appearance. Some sensors such as P-band can even detect soil moisture. It acted as a scanning tool of the Earth, penetrating most obstacles. The comparison of subsidence for two high-tech areas of the world provides a structural example of SAR’s capability. Future city planning and geological movement warnings can all be based on the data of SAR.
